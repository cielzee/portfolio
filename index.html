Storyline - ABC News

React / Java Spring - Pivotal Labs, New York & Santa Monica

Discovery & framing: Our client initially described the problem to us as "everyone on the news team gets too much email". Every news team employee was constanty managing a large daily influx of emails, because was the primary form of communication. Further adding to that load were the distribution email lists employees were included in, some that were mandatory for all employees and others that were mandatory varying by department. We hypothesized that the real issue at hand was a workflow problem, and the emails were a symptom of it. I worked alongside 1 product designer, 1 product manager, and 1 other developer, as well as our client stakeholders, to validate our problem. We started by identifying 3 distinct demographics of our users - cutting producers, senior producers, and field correspondents - and getting a comprehensive understanding of their day to day activities and interactions by going through a detailed service blueprint. We identified at which points in each of our persona's days we thought communication became hard for our users to manage. After conducting user research with 15 news team employees fitting our personas and synthesizing data from our interviews, key insights began to surface.

Problem: The news team manages all aspects of news production - from initial pitch, to assets, drafts, and final cuts - over email. This makes it hard for anyone involved to manage changes and updates to the story, and also limits visibility to only those working on that story. An important email could easily be missed, or a relevant person be accidentally excluded from an update, and a lot of work was being duplicated.

Solution: Our solution was a collaborative web application centered around stories, the core product of the news team. News employees would be able to see a list of all stories currently in progress, and close out stories when they were finished. Employees would be given permissions to edit and take action on stories they were assigned to, allowing them to add comments, assets, and include other employees.

Product: An internal tool to help manage the news team's daily workflow

- Implemented client features in React, mentored client developers in agile process and test driven development
- Java Spring / PostGRES to store data, no external integrations

Palat

MeteorJS - Personal project

Palat
Product: my vision of a solution to the problems I experience when using yelp. A food review app that utilizes positive reinforcement from users and constructive feedback and in turn, fosters a more positive business relationship with restaurants.

There are 2 problems I perceive with Yelp's user experience, splitting user groups by restaurant goers (consumers) and restaurant owners. Personally, as a consumer, when I look at a restaurant's Yelp page, I'm trying to determine what to order and what other people usually order at this restaurant. This information isn't easily visible because of the way Yelp structures its rating/review system. A restaurant's ratings and public perception could be potentially skewed by negative reviews that usually have more to do with the service the reviewer recieved than the food itself. My solution is an app where dishes are ranked within a restaurant by a 'like' system, allowing the most popular dishes to float to the top of the page. The restaurant itself can be rated on points such as service, quality, price, etc., but these ratings would be separated from the dishes they offer.

On the other hand, many small businesses have negative relationships with Yelp due to Yelp's business model. Yelp uses its ranking system as a way to collect revenue from businesses, and some businesses refuse to pay to filter negative reviews or their search ranking and consequently see their profile negatively affected. Chefs especially perceive Yelp to be a one way relationship, where users passive aggressively vent out negative feedback. Businesses are not given the opportunity to respond to reviewers unless they purchase an additional features from Yelp. A compilation of complaints from the FTC revealed that 'the most common complaints claim that Yelp filters out positive reviews and allows negative reviews through, especially in cases where the vendor in question did not purchase advertising.' If that's not a flawed business model, I don't know what is. I'd like to see a system in which reviews of a restaurant's food are truly reflective of what they offer, and a more transparent business model that doesn't rely on fraudulent reviews.
